---
title: 'We build for agents now'
description: 'The line between what a person made and what a machine made is no longer distinguishable or meaningful.'
pubDate: 'Jun 20 2026'
---

I wrote this with an agent. I drafted, it argued back, I rewrote, it rewrote, and the more it argued and I rewrote, the more my brain turned to mush, and by the end I could not reliably tell you which sentences started with me and which started with it. That is basically the point. The line we keep trying to draw, between what a person made and what a machine made, has stopped describing how the work actually gets done, and once you have watched it happen in your own writing, you start seeing it everywhere.

The best agents already produce work indistinguishable from a competent person's, and the rest are closing the gap. We keep trying to make them declare themselves anyway, with a flag or a disclosure or a badge, and the bad actors won't comply, which everyone knows. But I don't think I'm a bad actor, and I don't follow the rules either. The representative case now is a person working through an agent, editing the output and sending it, and there is no true answer to whether a human or a machine produced it.

Once you accept that, the gates you had been assuming start to look naive. We tell ourselves that humans live in the UI and agents live in the API, so we can build for each separately. But an agent uses whatever gets it what it needs most cheaply, and often enough that is the UI, clicking the same buttons our users click, because the button is where the thing it wants lives. You don't get to choose which door it comes through. You only get to choose whether the room behind it was built for it.

The same thing happens with pricing. You can't charge an actor for being a human or an agent when you can't tell which it is, so every cheaper lane you open for agents becomes the lane everyone quietly routes through. What you can still see, and still price, is what gets consumed. The identity of the actor was never going to be one of those things (or if it is, it's probably a question for the accounts team, not product).

So we build for agents. Not as a separate user segment, but as the default assumption about who is on the other end, with no privileged human-only path quietly holding things together, because we can't defend it.

What that asks of the software is more than we have been used to giving. For as long as you could assume a competent person on the other end you could, occasionally, be a little sloppy and get away with it. Your friendly human user mostly covered for you: they read the ambiguous affordance the way you meant it, worked around the confusing default, never chose the option that was obviously a mistake. That judgement sat in the user, and it quietly subsidised every gap you left in the product. You could let a single maintainer make a popular open source repository private with no additional checks or confirmation, and trust that they *mostly* [wouldn't do that](https://httpie.io/blog/stardust). In return, you assumed good intent when they exceeded a limit you hadn't properly enforced, and gave them a pretty picture of a horse on a bicycle when they got through all their notifications, because they liked that.

An agent doesn't subsidise you. It does exactly what the interface says, at scale and at speed, so the affordance you left ambiguous gets used the wrong way ten thousand times and the bad default becomes the common case. And a hostile agent does the same thing on purpose. It used to take real effort to turn a weakness in your software into an attack: someone had to notice it and build the automation to exploit it, and that friction quietly protected you. A fleet of agents removes the friction. The gap an honest agent stumbles into is the gap a thousand hostile ones probe, cheaply and tirelessly, and they only have to be right once. Whatever judgement you didn't put into the software, something on the other side now has the patience to go looking for it.

The answer isn't rocket science. The way you serve a participant you can't see coming is the way you were always meant to serve the ones you could: clear defaults, contracts that mean what they say, surfaces shaped like the work rather than like the screen a person happens to read them on. The judgement that used to sit in our users and quietly cover for every gap has nowhere left to sit except the software. It is the stuff we have always known we should invest in and have often found a reason to delay, and (of course) it is the stuff that most needs the judgement of experienced humans on the other side of it, too.
