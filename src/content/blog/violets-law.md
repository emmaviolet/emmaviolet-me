---
title: "Violet's Law"
description: "The work that gets done is the work whose payoff you can see, which is almost never the work that matters most."
pubDate: "2026-06-25"
---

Last week my team shipped a small thing: a dropdown in the repository header that lets you jump straight to another repo, instead of trailing back to your dashboard and starting again. It is a good small thing, done well, and people noticed, which they almost never do. One called it the best thing GitHub had shipped in three years. Another wrote, simply, "you legend we love you so much". A third could not believe he was praising GitHub, and did it anyway, on the grounds that good design is usually just the obvious thing finally done. I am proud of it, and the love is deserved.

It also shipped in roughly the time it takes to read this paragraph, with no argument from anyone, because the moment you saw it you could see the point of it. The work I care about most is the opposite kind, and I have spent months pushing for it: the platform underneath the product, the layer everything else quietly sits on, the sort nobody will ever tweet about. Nobody disputes the engineering case. It loses anyway, because its value stays hidden until the moment you need it, the way this kind of work nearly always does. It matters more than the dropdown by an amount that would look like a typo if I wrote it down, and it is far harder to build besides; the only thing that ever separated their fates was that you could see the point of the dropdown at a glance, and the platform shows you nothing at all until the day it fails.

I used to think I was losing the argument for hard platform work on presentation, that one more chart would carry the room. It never did. I've decided to start calling this Violet's Law, on the grounds that naming a law after yourself is the cheapest advertising available to a person with a blog: the work that gets done is the work whose payoff you can see, which is almost never the work that matters most.

I meet the Law most often in performance work, which is what I am on a stage about this week, next to Elena, who can open a flamegraph of the pull request page, a dense picture of where every millisecond goes, and find the slow one before you have finished sitting down. That is a real and difficult skill, and it is the part of performance work the field has mastered; the tools have caught up with her, and continuous profiling, exotic not long ago, is now a checkbox in Datadog or Grafana like any other. What none of them can tell you, Elena included, is which of those milliseconds anyone would have paid to keep. The graph answers its own question perfectly and stays silent on the only one that decides anything.

Everything that decides whether a slow thing gets fixed sits outside the graph, in the part with a person in it, and it is all the same job: making a value nobody can see legible to someone who needs to see it.

First, knowing what is worth fixing. The graph hands you the hundred slowest things on the page; it cannot tell you that ninety-nine are slow where nobody stands, and the hundredth is the one your biggest customer hits at eight in the morning while deciding whether to renew. The evidence that this matters is, for the record, enormous. Vodafone [ran two versions](https://web.dev/case-studies/vodafone) of a page identical but for a third faster load, and the faster one sold eight per cent more. Google once [slowed its own search](https://services.google.com/fh/files/blogs/google_delayexp.pdf) by four hundred milliseconds, below the floor of conscious perception, and the world searched less, and kept searching less after the delay was gone, as though four hundred milliseconds had quietly lowered its opinion of the place. You find the eight-in-the-morning customer the slow way, in the support ticket and the forwarded email, and you let what the wait cost them pick the target, rather than whatever was most satisfying to fix.

Second, being allowed to do it, which is the platform problem again. The work most worth doing tends to be the work whose payoff is hardest to draw, so the foundations rot politely while the visible wins ship on time. McKinsey reckons the [technical debt](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/mckinsey-digital/our-insights/breaking-technical-debts-vicious-cycle-to-modernize-your-business) the average company carries is worth twenty to forty per cent of its whole technology estate, which is a board-ready way of saying that a third of what you own is held together by things no one could fit on a slide. You do not win that argument with a better slide. You win it by carrying the customer into the room in the decider's language: "still slow for the account you mentioned, and we are close" buys you another week, where "ruled out three hypotheses" buys you nothing.

Third, the part after you ship, which I am worst at. You go back to the eight-in-the-morning customer and the forwarded email and say, slightly cringing, here is what was broken and here is what we did, because work nobody hears about is the first thing struck off the next list. There is a name for this kind of work now, [glue work](https://www.noidea.dog/glue), the invisible kind that holds a company upright and earns nobody a promotion, whose main reward is being trusted with more of it. Closing the loop is how you make the work visible enough to survive being cut next time.

None of the three is a skill you can put on a CV, which is exactly why the industry has quietly agreed they are someone else's job. The flamegraph will always tell you the truth about where the time goes. It has never once told me which of those truths was worth a week of my life, because that was never a question with a right answer. There was a person in it the whole time, just off the edge of the graph, and I spent ten years getting fluent in the graph because the graph never once asked me to go and find them.
